Four use cases surfaced.

One is the marquee. The others compound on the data it structures.

01 All-up IS budget ~1,200 hrs / cycle · ~50% addressable

July through October. ~100 person-hours a week across a 3,000-person IS organization, pulling from Excel, Oracle, technical-debt registers, lifecycle plans, revenue projections and shifting cost inputs — then rebuilt every time leadership pushes back. “Excel spreadsheet tracking, which I despise.”

Pattern Aggregate every input · generate a credible first cut · explain why. Your team edits something rather than creating from ground zero.
02 Project-level budget estimating 4–5 requests / week · already in motion

A cost-model database powers the “shopping cart” estimating tool today. The stretch goal: an end user names the clinic, lists the services, and a detailed project budget comes back — with the scope packet ready for the delivery team.

Pattern Same engine as #1, scoped down. The cost models exist; the unstructured project context doesn't. Mine intake → generate estimate → hand off scope.
03 Vendor & contract optimization Positive opex variance · quietly

Mid-year renegotiations are one of the three reasons IS runs in the black on opex — but the signal lives in PDFs, supplier emails, and Oracle line items, not in one place. Surface renewal windows, price drifts, and consolidation opportunities before the cycle, not after.

Pattern Structured data from unstructured sources — contracts, vendor mail, line items. Pulls renegotiation forward, not into the rear-view.
04 Peer benchmark analysis The Copilot story

“I asked Copilot why it chose those five. It said: you're right, I kind of made that up.” Providence is unusual — large Catholic, multi-state, west-coast — so picking peers is hard and the LLM alone can't be trusted with it. Grounded in third-party + your own data, with provenance, it can.

Pattern Once the budget is structured, this becomes a query, not a project. Citations on every comparable — no made-up peer sets.
How we'd start Pick #1. Scope in one session. Production-ready first cut in your hands in days, not months — nothing changes hands until it works against your data, in your environment.
